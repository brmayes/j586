Pricing Statistics of New Housing Over Time

The evolution of the prices of houses on the market nationally and by region

By Brittany Renee Mayes April 1, 2015

The pie graphs show the number of new single-family homes sold nationally by sales price range in 2003, 2008, and 2013. Over this ten year timespan, the prices of housing has increased and thus there is a steady showing of more expensive houses being sold. *Numbers may not equal 100% due to rounding.

In 2003, houses under $149,999 dictated a quarter of the houses that were sold. Additionally, the next price range, $150,000 to $199,999 was the second largest category with just under a quarter of houses being sold.
In 2008, the $150,000 to $199,999 price range took over as the leader of number of houses being sold in that range despite dropping to just over 21%. The under $149,999 dropped to thrid place after the $200,000 to $249,999 price range. This increase shows the progression of housing prices due to the fact that the distribution of houses sold increased in every category over the $150,000 to $199,999 price range.
In 2013, the under $149,999 dropped to the lowest number of houses being sold. There was an increase in distribution between ever other category. There is a drastic shift between 2003 where the lowest two categories shifted from being half of the total houses sold to 2013 where the same two categories equaled just over a quarter of the houses sold.

Prices of real estate in the United States are rising due to several reasons. The first of which includes the size of the house being built. As shown below, nationally between 1973 and 2013, there was over an 150% increase in average square foot per year.
